*A simple ruleset for hexcrawls.* 
In ideal conditions, a character or party of characters can move up to 3 miles in an hour, or 24 miles over an 8-hour day. 

## World Map 
On the World Map, one hex is twenty-four miles across. 
- A character can move one hex in a day's march along a road. 
- A character on horseback moves twice as fast when entering a road, plains or hills hex. 
- When entering a trackless wilderness hex, travel time is doubled. 
- When entering a mountain or forest hex, travel time is doubled. 


## Regional Map 
On a Regional Map, one hex is three miles across or a "league". All movement on this scale track is measured in hexes, not miles or hours. There's no such thing as traveling half a hex. For ideal results, a majority of hexes on your wilderness map should contain a feature of some kind. It doesn't have to be a dungeon, but consider adding landmarks, or other points of interest. When the party enters a hex, consult the chart below. A hex's type determines how much time it takes to travel into that hex. A character on horseback moves twice as fast when entering a road, plains or hills hex.

## Entering a Hex
When entering a forest, swamp, or mountain hex, the player leading the exploration must make a navigationcheck to determine if they get lost. You should set the DC depending on the difficulty of the navigation by a number between 10 and 20. 
- If there are landmarks, or other obvious clues, the DC should be on the lower end. 
- If the party is travelling from identical hex to another, it should be higher. 
 
Getting lost means the party instead travels to a different adjacent hex that's reasonable for them to end up in. If the party is travelling along a river, coastline or treeline. They should succeed navigation checks automatically. Once inside a hex, the DM might describe some notable feature the characters see. They might ask for a check to discover some hidden secrets that might not be immediately obvious to arriving characters, or simply ask which hex they are entering next. Similarly, navigation checks to follow tracks into an adjacent hex have advantage. 
### Trailblaze. 
Once a character has entered a hex by making a successful navigation check, they automatically succeed navigation checks made to enter that hex in the future. 

## Exploring a Hex 
A party can spend some time to thoroughly explore the hex they're in. Doing so takes the same amount of time as entering that hex would have, though being on horseback confers no benefits here, and reveals any hidden locations that might be in a hex. 

| Hex Type          | Time    |
| ----------------- | ------- |
| Road              | 1 hour  |
| Plains or Hills   | 2 hours |
| Forests or Swamps | 3 hours |
| Mountains         | 4 hours |

## Wilderness Day 
At the start of every day, the DM rolls for weather to determine any applicable modifiers to the day's navigation checks. A full day of exploration is 8 hours long, and any time spent exploring beyond that risks driving one or more members of the party to exhaustion. Whenever a party finishes entering or exploring a hex, if they have spent more than 8 hours exploring that day, tally up the additional hours spent and ask each character to make a Constitution saving throw. The DC is 10 + 1 for each hour past 8. Each character that fails suffers one level of exhaustion.

## Downtime Hours
Each day is split into 8 hours of exploration, and 8 hours of rest. That means there are 8 hours unaccounted for. For the purposes of this system, we assume 2 of those hours are consumed in the morning having breakfast, getting dressed and preparing for the day's exploring, and an additional 2 in the evening on making camp, eating dinner and generally winding down after a long day. That leaves 4 hours that a character can use as they wish, provided they didn't march beyond 8 hours. 

### Downtime activities
#### Forage for Food 
roll a survival check. A result of 10 provides you with enough food to forgo the use of one ration, and each increment of 5 above that allows you to replace an additional ration. 
#### Craft Items 
Make a crafting check. You must have the appropriate tools and setup to make progress on a crafting project. If the project requires checks to be subsequent, make sure you have enough time left in the day to finish the craft.
#### Scout Ahead 
Explore the surrounding area. The DM might ask for a survival or perception check to determine if you find something

## Excursion Resting 
A short rest can be taken once every 24 hours over the course of 8 hours, 6 of which must be spent sleeping and 2 of which may be light activity. Any rest is interrupted by the following: 
- Rolling initiative. 
- Casting a spell with a spell slot. 
- Taking damage. 
A long rest can only be gained by taking three consecutive short rests in the same location without interruption, or by resting for a single night in a safe haven. 

## Roll for Weather 
The following tables are used to determine the weather based on the month. Each weather has an effect next to it, imposing advantage or disadvantage on navigation and marching rolls. Feel free to adjust the calendar to fit yours. Marching refers to the constitution saving throws made when spending more than 8 hours exploring. Navigation refers to the rolls made to avoid getting lost, or when tracking something.
